The Election of 1933

On January 30, President Hindenburg named Adolf Hitler chancellor of Germany. Two days later, Hitler dissolved the Reichstag and declared new elections for March 5. The Nazi-led government quickly harnessed the resources of both party and state to terrorize its opponents and flood the public with its own political message.

Under such decidedly un-free and un-fair conditions, almost every single party lost support—unsurprisingly—except for the NSDAP, which increased its share of the national vote by 10% to 43.9%. This still left the Nazis far short of an outright majority in the Reichstag, however, despite their countless campaign advantages. The SPD, facing censorship and intimidation, managed to garner 18% of the national vote and perform particularly well in the north-central electoral districts of Magdeburg and South Hanover-Brunswick. The Z/BVP, encountering less harassment, performed only slightly worse than usual, holding onto 14% of the national share and maintaining its status as the top vote-getter in two heavily Catholic western districts. Incredibly, the KPD mustered 12.3%, even though the Nazis had arrested every one of its leaders who had not already fled into exile or gone underground. The DNVP, meanwhile, in alliance with a far-right veterans’ organization and in sympathy with the Nazis, drew 8%, the only other party to gain a significant share of the vote.

Only in tandem with its coalition partner, the DNVP, did the Nazi Party hold a majority of the Reichstag, but it wanted a 2/3 supermajority in order to pass a so-called “Enabling Act” that would suspend the legislative body and allow Hitler to rule by fiat. After unilaterally annulling the KPD’s 81 seats on March 8 and then cajoling Z/BVP support, the Nazis won passage of the Enabling Act in the Reichstag on March 23, by a vote of 441 to 94. Only the SPD voted against it. Not long thereafter, the Nazi government banned all other parties outright and stripped the Reichstag of its remaining power.
